\chapter{Research Questions Analysis}

The final integrated dataset contains 10,054 restaurants. In particular, 5,967
restaurants are available only on Google, 3,179 on Google and Tripadvisor, 163 on
Google and TheFork, and 745 on all three platforms. This difference in coverage
must be considered when comparing ratings, because the platforms do not describe
exactly the same subset of restaurants.

For restaurants with at least two platform ratings, the ratings are generally
close. The average difference between the highest and lowest rating is 0.518
stars; 65.1\% of restaurants differ by at most 0.5 stars and 89.6\% differ by at
most 1 star. The strongest agreement is between Google and TheFork, with an
average absolute difference of 0.246 stars. Comparisons involving Tripadvisor
show larger differences: 0.494 stars for Google--Tripadvisor and 0.482 stars for
Tripadvisor--TheFork. The most relevant result is therefore not that ratings are
unrelated, but that Tripadvisor tends to be the platform that creates the largest
cross-platform gaps.

Figure \ref{fig:rq-platform-bias} focuses on this platform effect. The plot uses
a leave-one-out (LOO) comparison: each platform is compared with the average
rating given by the other available platforms for the same restaurants. A
positive value means that the platform tends to rate restaurants higher than the
others; a negative value means that it tends to rate them lower. Tripadvisor is
the only platform clearly below zero, with a LOO value of -0.362 stars. Google
and TheFork are above zero, with values of 0.308 and 0.180 stars respectively.
This confirms that Tripadvisor is systematically more severe in the integrated
dataset, while Google and TheFork are more positive.

Data quality helps explain part of the disagreement between platforms. The most
relevant quality indicator in this analysis is the number of reviews supporting a
rating. Restaurants with fewer reviews show less stable evaluations. For
restaurants with fewer than 20 Google reviews, the average cross-platform rating
difference is 0.792 stars. The value decreases to 0.653 stars for restaurants
with 20--99 reviews, to 0.510 stars for restaurants with 100--499 reviews, and to
0.454 stars for restaurants with 500--1,999 reviews. Figure
\ref{fig:rq-popularity} shows this trend: as review volume increases, platform
ratings become more consistent.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{images/research_questions/11_q6.png}
\caption{Average rating difference by Google review-volume group. Restaurants with few reviews show stronger disagreement across platforms.}
\label{fig:rq-popularity}
\end{figure}

The effect of low review volume is not identical on every platform. On Google,
restaurants with fewer than 20 reviews have a lower average rating than
well-reviewed restaurants (4.215 vs 4.274). The same pattern appears on TheFork
(4.156 vs 4.460). Tripadvisor is different: low-review restaurants average 3.953,
while well-reviewed restaurants average 3.929. Therefore, few reviews do not
produce a single universal effect on ratings. The main issue is that ratings
based on a small number of reviews are less reliable and more likely to generate
large differences across platforms.

Geographic coverage is also uneven. Restaurants closer to the centre of Milan
are better represented on multiple platforms. In the central area, the average
number of platforms per restaurant is 1.67, compared with 1.53 in the periphery.
Tripadvisor covers 53.1\% of central restaurants and 43.3\% of peripheral
restaurants; TheFork covers 13.9\% of central restaurants and 9.3\% of
peripheral restaurants. Central restaurants also have more Google reviews, with a
median of 269 compared with 161 in peripheral areas.

Figure \ref{fig:rq-coverage-density} shows the density of restaurants that are
present on all three platforms. The colour scale is normalized from 0 to 1 and
represents local concentration. Blue and green areas indicate
lower local density, while yellow, orange and red indicate progressively higher
density. The red area therefore identifies the part of Milan where restaurants
with complete three-platform coverage are most concentrated. The map shows that
this richest coverage is mainly located in the central part of the city.

Restaurant category also affects both rating level and rating consistency.
Figure \ref{fig:rq-cuisine} compares cuisine types using two values: the average
rating and the average difference between platforms. Pizza, American and
fast-food restaurants have lower average ratings and larger cross-platform
differences. Pizza restaurants have an average rating of 3.957 and an average
difference of 0.774 stars; American restaurants have an average rating of 3.787
and an average difference of 0.806 stars. Japanese and Indian restaurants are
instead more stable: Japanese restaurants average 4.318 with a difference of
0.412 stars, while Indian restaurants average 4.256 with a difference of 0.335
stars.

The plot reports both \textit{Italiana} and \textit{Italiano}. These are not two
substantially different cuisine groups; they are two native labels preserved from
the source vocabularies after integration. Since the analytical table keeps the
platform-provided cuisine labels, some semantically equivalent categories remain
separate. For interpretation, both labels should be read as Italian cuisine. This
is a residual vocabulary-normalization limitation, and a further semantic
standardization of cuisine labels would be a useful improvement.

Price level shows a more moderate pattern. Restaurants in the lowest price tier
have an average rating of 4.123 and an average cross-platform difference of
0.515 stars. Restaurants in price tier 3 have a higher average rating, 4.332, and
a lower difference, 0.379 stars. This suggests that higher-price restaurants tend
to receive slightly better and more consistent ratings, although the number of
restaurants is not the same across price tiers.

Platform presence is another important result. Restaurants that appear on more
platforms are generally more visible and more reviewed. Google-only restaurants
have an average Google rating of 4.242 and a median of 83 Google reviews.
Restaurants present on two platforms have an average Google rating of 4.283 and a
median of 371 reviews. Restaurants present on all three platforms reach an
average Google rating of 4.355 and a median of 548 reviews. Figure
\ref{fig:rq-platform-coverage} shows that wider platform coverage is associated
with higher visibility and slightly higher ratings.

Finally, photo counts mostly describe platform activity rather than restaurant
quality. On Tripadvisor, the relationship between number of photos and number of
reviews is strong, equal to 0.783. The relationship between photos and rating is
much weaker, equal to 0.100. Google shows the same direction, with 0.212 between
photos and reviews and 0.116 between photos and rating. Photos therefore provide
useful information about visibility and engagement, but they should not be
interpreted as a direct measure of restaurant quality.

Overall, the integrated database shows that online restaurant ratings in Milan
are usually similar across platforms, but not identical. Tripadvisor tends to be
more severe than Google and TheFork; restaurants with few reviews show greater
rating instability; central and highly visible restaurants are better covered;
and cuisine, price and platform presence all contribute to explaining rating
differences. The integration of the three sources therefore makes it possible to
identify not only average restaurant reputation, but also the cases where that
reputation depends strongly on the platform and on the quality of the available
data.
